# Mentorship as a Catalyst: Exploring Support Systems in MSW Graduate Training

## 📋 CEU Information & Session Disclosures
* **Presenter:** Matthew Monfils, Department of Social Work, Grand View University
* **Session Length:** 90 Minutes Total (Interleaved Framework: 60-Min Total Lecture / 30-Min Total Dialogue)
* **Institutional Approval:** Approved under Grand View University IRB #405-26
* **Ethical Compliance:** This workshop strictly adheres to the National Association of Social Workers (NASW) Code of Ethics, specifically leveraging core values of **Social Justice**, **Competence**, and the **Importance of Human Relationships**.

### 📝 Workshop Abstract
This presentation explores the critical role of mentorship in Master of Social Work (MSW) education. Grounded in critical pedagogy, the study examines how formal and informal mentoring relationships influence professional identity and academic persistence among emerging practitioners. Findings highlight the need for equitable, developmental support systems that bridge the gap between theory and practice, especially for underserved students.

### 🎯 Session Learning Objectives
By the conclusion of this 90-minute continuing education block, attendees will be able to:
1. **Analyze** the impact of formal and informal mentorship on the development of professional identity and academic persistence in emerging social work practitioners.
2. **Create** evidence-based recommendations for designing developmental, rather than administrative, mentorship models in academic and field settings.
3. **Discuss** the reciprocal benefits of the mentoring relationship for both the emerging practitioner (mentee) and the experienced practitioner (mentor).

### 🟦 Block 1: Context & The Knowledge Gap (0–22 Minutes)
* **15 Minutes — Speaking Session:** The national landscape of MSW education. Introducing the systemic trend where traditional mentoring programs operate reactively, creating a thin operational margin of error that forces vulnerable students to take on personal and professional risk alone.
* **7 Minutes — Interactive Dialogue:** **Activity 1 — Disruption Analysis**
  * *Dialectical Prompt:* Reflect on a time your professional guidance or supervision was strictly administrative/logistical rather than developmental. What core structural pieces were missing?
  * *Empirical Context:* Participant B noted that navigating a university can feel like navigating a rigid, corporate organization where support is limited to transaction points like *"admissions, commencement, or getting forms finalized"*. 
  * *Critical Pedagogy Peer-Level Rule:* The presenter will step away from the podium and join a random attendee group or mobile chat-thread as a peer, sharing their own historical developmental vulnerabilities on equal ground.

### 🟦 Block 2: Theoretical Grounding & Frameworks (22–45 Minutes)
* **15 Minutes — Speaking Session:** Deep dive into Critical Pedagogy (Freire, Giroux, hooks). Deconstructing the "banking concept" of education (where mentors deposit facts into passive mentees) and replacing it with problem-posing dialogue, collaborative consciousness, and transformative praxis.
* **8 Minutes — Interactive Dialogue:** **Activity 2 — Problem-Posing Reframe**
  * *Dialectical Prompt:* Look at the current workflow errors or barriers on your device. Frame a common structural failure in your field as a systemic institutional flaw rather than an individual character deficiency.
  * *Empirical Context:* Participant D encountered acute structural friction from communication latency when an urgent family emergency conflicted with programmatic timelines. Critical pedagogy demands reframing this as an institutional accessibility design failure rather than a student lifestyle deficiency.
  * *Critical Pedagogy Peer-Level Rule:* The presenter will actively submit their own real-time reframe text into the shared pool alongside attendees, relinquishing the role of evaluator or grading moderator.

---

### 🟦 Block 3: Empirical Findings & Lived Realities (45–67 Minutes)
* **15 Minutes — Speaking Session:** Direct presentation of qualitative data from our five semi-structured interviews. Identifying why students prioritize faculty contact as a vital source of developmental, psychosocial mentoring rather than simple administrative task checking.
* **7 Minutes — Interactive Dialogue:** **Activity 3 — Solidarity Mapping**
  * *Dialectical Prompt:* Review the informal survival networks professionals organically build via messaging apps to combat burnout. How can we structurally design formal environments to mirror this raw peer solidarity?
  * *Empirical Context:* Our data shows that non-traditional and remote students build strong, horizontal communication lines (*"texting classmates"*) to fill formal information voids, bypassing the traditional "Fishbowl Effect" where open vulnerability is complicated by public online audio setups.
  * *Critical Pedagogy Peer-Level Rule:* The presenter acts exclusively as a co-collaborator in logging brainstorming ideas, intentionally refusing to judge, rank, or police the baseline assumptions of the responses.

---

### 🟦 Block 4: Reciprocal Benefits & Strategic Roadmap (67–90 Minutes)
* **15 Minutes — Speaking Session:** Transitioning from theory to actionable praxis. Building our multi-level roadmap: Micro (credit-bearing mentorship checkpoints embedded in first-year curricula), Mezzo (near-peer matching to alleviate faculty communication loads), and Macro policy (demanding explicit regulatory accessibility metrics from programmatic bodies like the CSWE).
* **8 Minutes — Interactive Dialogue:** **Activity 4 — Mapping the Mutual Growth Loop**
  * *Dialectical Prompt:* Design an intentional professional feedback loop where an experienced practitioner (mentor) explicitly gains fresh critical pedagogical insights from an emerging practitioner (mentee). How does this mutual growth sustain longevity for both parties?
  * *Empirical Context:* Participant A noted that tracking a mentor's active career choices builds an immediate sense of long-term professional destination, creating rapid-response communication windows (*"less than 24 hours"*) that directly lower practitioner anxiety and elevate systemic persistence.
  * *Critical Pedagogy Peer-Level Rule:* The presenter documents their personal professional growth derived from listening to the audience members today, validating the community as a co-creator of academic knowledge.
