This study explores how first-year Master of Social Work (MSW) students experience

mentorship at a small private university, addressing the gap between the recognized value of

mentorship and its lack of formal implementation. Grounded in the theoretical framework of

critical pedagogy, the research views mentorship as a collaborative tool for professional

empowerment and social justice. A qualitative research design was employed, utilizing semi-

structured interviews with a purpose sample of five MSW students. The findings reveal that

mentorship currently functions as an informal, faculty-dependent, and peer-driven experience.

Students identified professors as primary navigators of professional identity, placing a high

importance on responsive communication and career role modeling. However, non-traditional

and remote students face significant barriers, often acting as their own blockers due to competing

life responsibilities and a lack of integrated support. The study highlights critical implications for

social work education, advocating for a shift from reactive to proactive support models. Key

recommendations include embedding mentorship in the required curriculum, establishing formal

near-peer mentoring programs to alleviate faculty burden, and adopting national accreditation

standards for mentorship accessibility. By shifting the burden of support from individual students

to the institution, MSW programs can foster a more equitable and sustainable environment for

emerging social work professionals.

Mentorship as a Catalyst: Exploring Support Systems in Master of Social Work Graduate

Training

Master of Social Work (MSW) academic programs at colleges and universities in the

United States involve a diverse blend of learning experiences. Across academic settings and field

placements, a wide variety of lessons unfold as theories are applied. Another channel for learning

is mentorship, a relationship that promotes collaboration and the pursuit of growth goals. This

helps bridge the gap between theory and practice in social work.

Background of the Problem

Mentorship is an aspect of social work education that risks being overlooked. Although

mentorship in the social work field is widely recognized as particularly important for

professional development and training, there is no explicit requirement or obligation to provide

it, and students miss out on its potential when opportunities are not offered through school

faculty or field supervisors (Pomeroy & Steiker, 2011). A lack of formal, structured mentorship

systems in MSW programs can leave emerging social work professionals without adequate

career development support.

A few definitions of key concepts help explore the issue of MSW mentorship. Mentoring is a

relationship in which a more experienced social worker (the mentor) nurtures the growth of a

less experienced person (the mentee) by supporting their professional and psychosocial

development (Skrzypek et al., 2019). Evidence-based practices also play a core role in social

work. This refers to a five-step process in social work practice that includes formulating

questions, searching for evidence, engaging in critical thinking, factoring evidence into practice,

and measuring outcomes (Weller et al., 2022). Distributed learning is another relevant topic, as

some programs feature a nontraditional format. In this method, online or hybrid educational

4

programs increase access for students who are from historically underserved backgrounds

(Jensen, 2017). Finally, near-peer mentoring deserves attention as a mentorship model in which

more advanced students (e.g., doctoral/MSW students) mentor students earlier in their education

(MSW/bachelor’s students) (Schuman et al., 2021).

Numbers are available to describe the possible scope of this problem. According to the 2023-

2024 Council on Social Work Education (CSWE) annual survey of social work programs, 225

MSW programs responding to the survey reported the following demographic metrics that reflect

the extent of who may be affected by this concern (CSWE, 2025):

  55,935 MSW students enrolled in the academic year.

  2,471 students who are first-generation at the master’s level.

  60.8% full-time students, and 39.2% part-time students.

  81.4% female students, 15.0% male, 3.1% unknown, and 0.5% other.

Although some MSW departments have formal mentoring programs, there is little evidence

that they are a standard feature. As recently as 2019, one formal mentoring program

implemented had no precedent for the model design (Skrzypek et al., 2019). This reflects a

couple of underlying problems. First, there is a lack of understanding related to structured

formats that can be used to provide mentorship to students. There is a knowledge gap in the

design, implementation, and evaluation of such programs. Second, the current momentum of this

problem leans toward maintaining the status quo.

As the issue stands today, the trend will likely be for many MSW programs to continue

relying on the traditional approach to student mentorship. This emphasizes informal mentorship

by faculty and field supervisors. Resource and time constraints can limit these individuals'

capacity to provide mentorship. This can be an unreliable approach, as it lacks equity due to

5

inconsistent access. It has been observed that up to half of the students in this setting lack a

mentor (Skrzypek et al., 2019; Stafford & Robbins, 1991). Also, even when it is provided,

mentorship guidance can be more administrative than developmental, given the status quo.

Without intentional effort to address these issues, a gap in professional development will persist,

especially for MSW students from historically underserved backgrounds. Students who are at

risk academically, in an online/distributed format, male, first-generation, and minority students

are disproportionately affected (Jensen, 2017; Rogers, 2013; Schuman et al., 2021).

Purpose of the Study

The purpose of this study is to explore how first-year MSW students think about and

experience mentorship at a small private university. Examining their feedback can inform the

design and implementation of formal mentorship models to support their professional

development, meet academic expectations, and address the needs of students from diverse

backgrounds. By engaging in further study, this research will guide recommendations for better

supporting students through the integration of theory and practice in mentorship.

Research Question

The research question to consider is: “To what extent does the presence of a mentorship

relationship, whether formal or informal, influence the development of professional identity and

academic persistence of first-year MSW students at a small private university?”

Considering the value of mentorship, this study aims to explore ways to make mentorship

more consistently accessible to MSW students. Seeking to understand the perceptions and

experiences of first-year MSW students at a small private university will help identify specific

Overview of Relevant Theory

Critical pedagogy, a theoretical framework in education and social change, first appeared

in the literature several decades ago. It challenges traditional approaches to education, which can

be seen as channels for social inequality and power imbalances, by addressing biases through a

collaborative, inclusive learning environment (Billings, 2024). In addition to the original

concepts, the theory has been interpreted in various settings over time and combined with other

theoretical frameworks.

Pedagogy of the Oppressed (1970), the foundational text on critical pedagogy by Paulo

Freire, was first published in English in 1970, after its original publication in 1968. Freire

centered their theory on several key ideas drawn from their experience teaching literacy in

Brazil. Core concepts include the following: critical consciousness, recognizing one’s reality as

an oppressive one to be addressed; education through problem-posing rather than banking, which

involves empowering dialogue instead of simply receiving and storing information; praxis, the

pursuit of social change through reflection and action; and education as a mutual process of

learning, understanding, and trust (Freire, 1970).

Over time, other theorists built on the framework established by Freire. Elevating the

importance of dialogue within critical pedagogy, Giroux (1985) reinforced the value of

knowledge through personal experience, noting its unique teaching potential when it is brought

into question. Intersectionality theory added to critical pedagogy by emphasizing factors such as

race, gender, and socioeconomic status and their role in the educational process (Hooks, 1994).

Applying these various parts of the theoretical framework creates an approach to learning that

8

serves as a vehicle for social justice (Billings, 2024). The core concepts encourage lessons

learned through action. For students being taught, critical pedagogy provides a method to

achieve the goal of emancipation, which is the ability to shape history through self-

determination, social knowledge, and self-knowledge (Kridel, 2010).

Other Studies Applying This Theory

Hall (2015) is a more recent study that employed critical pedagogy with mentorship.

Critical pedagogy is discussed as a conceptual framework with multiple benefits for a

mentorship program, including: improving students' personal agency, challenging the status quo,

addressing power imbalances, achieving social justice, improving cultural relevance, and

focusing on strengths-based interventions (Hall, 2015). In examining a mentorship program for

African American adolescent males grounded in critical pedagogy, this study found that this

approach was a culturally responsive intervention for developing aptitude, cultural identity, and

educational competence.

Lorenzetti et al. (2019) further evaluated the development and implementation of a

mentorship program through the lens of critical pedagogy. This study focused on transformative

learning for MSW students through relationships with community-based mentors. The results

showed the benefits of a mentorship program framed by critical pedagogy. Relationships with

mentors were found to involve acceptance, friendliness, encouragement, and motivation, and

students gained experience and knowledge in critical dialogue, community development, and

social justice (Lorenzetti et al., 2019).

Relevance of Theory to Current Study

In this study, critical pedagogy serves as a conceptual framework for understanding

mentorship in an empowering way. Through this perspective, the role of mentorship in social

9

work education is collaborative, inclusive, and capable of addressing power imbalances and

social inequalities. Critical pedagogy frames the mentor-mentee relationship as a mutual process

of education, dialogue, and social justice.

This theoretical lens also informs the research question and design. Rather than seeing

professional identity and academic persistence as only individual achievements, they can be

explored as part of the larger systems around them. Approaching qualitative research through

interviews embodies the principles of critical pedagogy by valuing knowledge derived from

personal experience. Using this framework also highlights the importance of equity, which can

be factored in by identifying barriers to success for students.

Critical pedagogy will also guide the interpretation of the findings. Mentorship will be

evaluated for its ability to promote personal agency and challenge the status quo of traditional

programs and systems. An emphasis on transformative learning will also inform the findings by

seeking mentorship strategies for development rather than relying solely on administrative

guidance. Employing critical pedagogy in this study will also help advocate for policy changes

that improve organizational standards and systems through mentorship.

Overview of Previous Research

The valuable role of mentorship in social work education has been well documented in

the literature. This is even though mentorship is not explicitly addressed in the Council on Social

Work Education’s Educational Policy and Accreditation Standards (Schuman et al., 2021). Some

of its critical functions include promoting professional growth, developing skills, and providing

personal support for MSW students in the academic environment and field placements (Skrzypek

et al., 2019).

The research shows the various effects of MSW mentorship programs. Across all

approaches, a pattern of significant benefits and practical challenges emerges, with these

challenges manifesting in various ways depending on context and setting (Jensen, 2017;

Schuman et al., 2021; Weller et al., 2022). The literature has adequately described this general

trend by evaluating mentorship efforts over time.

Building on prior studies, the discussion goes beyond merely demonstrating the value of

mentorship. The focus also shifts to how program design and implementation influence benefits

and to the challenges that must be considered in the setting. Various factors, organized around

key themes, include the theoretical basis of programs, the models implemented, the benefits

sought by students and institutions, and barriers to success. The research takes these variables

into account and makes recommendations for future consideration.

Key Themes in the Literature

11

Across a common theme, the literature consistently demonstrates a wide range of benefits

of mentorship in MSW programs, including academic and professional success. The specific

benefits observed depend on the characteristics of the academic institution and its goals in

designing and implementing a mentorship program. Formal programs have demonstrated a

variety of positive academic and professional outcomes. General themes include improved

grades, higher retention and graduation rates, greater self-efficacy, and stronger professional

identity (Jensen, 2017; Schuman et al., 2021). Another example is a program that increased

students’ use of the evidence-based practice process, thereby improving research competence

(Weller et al., 2022).

Another benefit of MSW mentorship is psychosocial support and socialization. Mentors

provide personal support, such as encouragement, confidence-building, and a safe space for

reflecting on challenges (Skrzypek et al., 2019). Benefits such as these are especially important

for non-traditional students, including older students, first-generation students, and those

managing additional obligations, such as work and family, as they often encounter greater

barriers to success (Jensen, 2017; Schuman et al., 2021). Mentoring can also serve a special role

for other students who may be considered non-traditional in social work, such as males (Rogers,

2013). An additional factor is helping students navigate the complex nature of higher education,

which can involve elements that are not explicit or openly addressed, which improves a sense of

belonging and community (Skrzypek et al., 2019).

Within the theme of the benefits of MSW mentorship programs, a final aspect is mutual

benefit. A consistent observation of successful mentoring is that the relationship is often

reciprocal, rather than benefiting the student alone (Lorenzetti et al., 2019). According to

feedback, mentors also report benefits from the mentorship experience, including a sense of

12

satisfaction, a renewed professional identity, skill development, alumni reconnecting with their

academic institution, and contributions to the profession (Skrzypek et al., 2019).

A second overarching theme in the literature is the range of models used in MSW

mentorship programs, along with the unique advantages and challenges of each. Some models

emphasize formal structure, such as student-alumni mentoring or peer-based mentoring. These

more formal programs are widely endorsed as a means for all students to access mentorship

equitably, compared with other forms of mentoring that can have drawbacks associated with

more organic, informal relationships (Jensen, 2017; Mitschke et al., 2021; Skrzypek et al., 2019).

While very promising, this approach faces challenges due to the substantial time, effort, and

resources required to design and implement it. E-mentoring, which involves mentorship

delivered electronically via email, phone, and virtual meetings, offers an effective alternative

with proven results for students who are geographically dispersed or at risk, as technology helps

bridge distances and address scheduling conflicts (Jensen, 2017; Mitschke et al., 2021; Schuman

et al., 2021). A final category of model, near-peer and nested mentoring, involves advanced

MSW or doctoral students mentoring other students. Advantages include leveraging shared

experiences and collaborative learning, as well as mentor leadership development (Inoue et al.,

2017; Schuman et al., 2021).

The final theme identified in existing research concerns the challenges of implementing a

successful MSW mentorship program. There is a risk of a lack of clarity and structure in

programs, which can lead to ambiguity, role confusion between mentors and mentees, and

insufficient commitment (Jensen, 2017; Skrzypek et al., 2019). In addition to time and resource

constraints, specific problems include uncertainty about how to communicate and what to

discuss, missed opportunities, and even the discontinuation of the mentoring relationship (Lee et

13

al., 2026; Skrzypek et al., 2019).

Gaps and Limitations

Limitations in the literature on MSW mentorship programs concern measurement and the

generalizability of studies. Much of the research involves small, homogeneous samples and

cross-sectional data (Jensen, 2017; Schuman et al., 2021; Weller et al., 2022). This results in

shortcomings such as limited generalizability across settings and contexts, a restricted ability to

move beyond correlation to examine causality, and a lack of understanding of the long-term

impacts of programs on students, mentors, and academic institutions.

Gaps in the research point to recommendations for future research to further develop the

literature on the subject. Examples of opportunities for future studies include making

observations over longer periods and recruiting a more diverse sample. Specific suggestions

from the literature can also guide this effort. Some ideas noted speak to the need for a deeper

examination of the mentoring relationship and of the mentors themselves. Approaches to address

these gaps in the research include using more comprehensive measures of mentorship quality and

studying mentors' experiences more directly (Jensen, 2017; Weller et al., 2022).

Summary and Transition

Existing research on mentorship in MSW programs highlights its significant value in

social work education, particularly for student retention, academic success, and professional

identity development. Both traditional formats, which center on face-to-face encounters, and

virtual formats, which rely more on digital communication, offer promising strategies when

paired with formal structures and a commitment to addressing barriers. Effective communication

of clear guidelines can foster supportive relationships among MSW students with diverse needs.

Methodology

The methodology for this study is designed to examine the experiences and perspectives

of research participants. Direct interactions are employed with structured data collection

procedures to gather a detailed understanding. Following these steps systematically promotes

findings grounded in real-world observations and upholds academic and ethical standards.

Research Design

This study uses a qualitative research design to obtain detailed descriptions of the

experiences of first-year MSW students. Social interactions and personal perspectives are

especially informative. In such a qualitative research design, the focus is on individuals'

experiences and their surrounding environment. The method employed is semi-structured

interviewing, which provides a flexible framework for exploring mentorship. The use of a

qualitative research design, specifically semi-structured interviewing, on the subject of

mentorship in an MSW program is supported by prior studies in social work education (Miehls et

al., 2013; Mitschke et al., 2021).

This approach to the research centers around open-ended interview questions with

follow-up for clarification as needed. Questions and answers explored in this way encourage

patterns and themes to emerge from participants' experiences. Rather than developing a

15

hypothesis in advance and testing it, semi-structured interviews can reveal unique observations

that might otherwise be difficult or impossible to discover. The personal and subjective nature of

academic and professional experiences calls for a qualitative research method, as numerical data

alone may not capture the rich descriptions needed to interpret people’s circumstances. Examples

of useful information that may be gathered include lived experiences in an individual’s own

words, consideration of a person’s surrounding environment, and an understanding of the

complexity and nuances of support systems.

Participants

This research employs a purposive sample of graduate students who are currently

enrolled in an academic MSW program. The target population includes students at a small,

private Midwestern university in the United States who are pursuing professional graduate

training. Within this population, the sample comes from current first-year MSW students who

can personally speak to relevant recent experiences. The first academic year is a critical period

for professional identity and academic persistence for these individuals and an ideal target for

mentorship efforts to address gaps in support.

Inclusion criteria require that participants be currently enrolled in the MSW program,

ensuring they have some experience engaging with faculty and peers. Exclusion criteria exclude

students outside the MSW department. The exclusions also require that students be within their

first year of study, which narrows the focus to the early phases of social work education, and that

students not be in the 3+2 program for the same reason, since this five-year MSW program

blends the undergraduate and graduate student experience into a single program. Following these

criteria, the research design reduces the number of variables related to first-year adjustment and

provides a homogenous group for comparison.

16

Recruitment of the purposive sample was performed through the MSW program director.

The director identified 16 students who met the eligibility criteria for participation and sent an

email to their student email addresses describing the study’s purpose. Although participation was

encouraged, it was voluntary, and no participants were mandated. Interested students responded

to the program director, who then provided a warm introduction to the researcher in a separate

email.

Data Collection Procedures

The semi-structured interview method explored participants' detailed experiences through

a consistent series of open-ended questions. Unlike other methods, such as surveys, this approach

offers a less rigid option that addresses the sometimes complex nature of academic and

professional training. Past research in similar circumstances demonstrates the appropriate use of

this process (Miehls et al., 2013; Mitschke et al., 2021).

A formal review and approval of the data collection procedures by the Grand View

University Institutional Review Board (IRB) established and maintained ethical research. The

project (IRB #405-26) was granted expedited review status (see Appendix A). This category was

warranted since the data collection involved voice recordings and interviews that posed only

minimal risk to participants.

Before any data was collected, the researcher provided participants with a formal

informed consent form (see Appendix B). Informed consent was obtained by the following steps:

walking participants through the study’s purpose, risks, and benefits; ensuring participants

understood that participation was voluntary; and securing a physical or electronic signature. A

deliberate effort to educate participants about the research and the details of their involvement

upheld ethical standards.

17

Participant privacy and safety factored into the research protocol. Privacy was protected

by storing all confidential data on a secure, password-protected computer. Procedures also

accounted for the confidentiality of participants. Identifiable information, such as names on

consent forms, was separated from the interview transcripts to ensure that no individual could be

traced back to their specific responses. As stated on the informed consent form, any presentation

of research findings is conducted in a way that prevents any individual’s identity from being

connected to the data or revealing them as a participant in the research.

The data collection process was done through a five-step process:

1.  Recruitment: The MSW program director identified eligible first-year students and sent a

recruitment email.

2.  Introduction: Interested students were introduced to the researcher by email to schedule

interview sessions.

3.  Preparation: Each interview was conducted using a standardized interview guide to

ensure all key research questions were addressed (see Appendix C).

4.  Interview sessions: Five interviews were conducted, lasting up to 30 minutes each. These

were conducted in private, student-accessible rooms on the university campus or via

video conferencing.

5.  Documentation: The interviews were recorded and transcribed into interview transcripts

using Otter AI (see Appendix D).

Following these data collection procedures, the qualitative data gathered were of high quality and

obtained ethically. Other research materials, such as best practices used and recruitment emails,

are securely maintained in the university data system and are outside the scope of the appendix.

Data Analysis

18

The data analysis was conducted using a four-step qualitative approach designed to

translate participants' responses into meaningful themes. This process is detailed in the interview

coding analysis worksheets (see Appendix E). Using the interview transcripts, the research

employed an inductive approach, basing its findings on participants' responses rather than testing

predetermined theories or hypotheses. The following four steps were applied to each of the five

interview questions:

1.  Immersive reading: The researcher read straight through all of the interview transcripts to

get a general overview of the content.

2.  Pattern identification: The researcher read through the transcripts a second time, noting

key words and phrases, and spotting patterns of content.

3.  Refining patterns: The researcher organized the content into themes observed between

participants.

4.  Detailed breakdown: The researcher identified additional patterns in the themes, which

served as more specific subthemes, and noted unique responses from individual

participants that stood out from the rest of the interview content.

This thematic coding process identified key terms, categorized major themes, developed nuanced

subthemes, and analyzed unique responses to move from what was said (descriptive) to what it

means (interpretive). Following these steps, three themes were identified, each with three

subthemes and multiple unique responses with specific details.

Institutional Guidance and Faculty Accessibility

The first theme focuses on the role of university staff and professors as a primary

navigator for students. Professors were often named as sources of mentorship. Support for

19

students in this form can extend beyond academic and professional help. Psychosocial support

and guidance on balancing personal life can also contribute to student success.

A first subtheme with guidance and accessibility is faculty as primary contacts.

Participants consistently identified professors as their first point of contact for both academic and

professional growth. Participant A noted that they “leaned on” a specific professor for support,

while Participant C described one as “fabulous” to interact with as their “number one person.”

The second subtheme is responsive communication. The speed of communication was

often cited as an important factor in student confidence. Of the good experiences shared by

Participant A, they described the organizational culture as “spot on,” with an emphasis on

responses often arriving in “less than 24 hours.” Participant C repeated this observation,

reporting that their go-to professor typically gets back to students “within the same day.”

A final subtheme is accounting for both administrative and developmental support.

Guidance often bridged the gap between logistics and career aspiration. There was some

variation in how participants reflected on their desires for mentorship. Participant B went into

detail about navigating the university as an organization, using formal terms for key parts of their

experience, such as “admissions” and “commencement,” and getting tasks “finalized.” In a

different light, Participant A looked to faculty for role modeling, stating how they turned to a

specific professor because their career is “something that I want to do.”

The Non-Traditional Student Experience

The second theme highlights the complex experience of students who balance multiple

roles and their competing responsibilities. This subject influenced participants' experiences in

areas such as connection with peers, program accessibility, managing multiple workloads, and

dealing with uncertain circumstances.

20

A first subtheme for non-traditional students is balancing multiple identities. Most

participants described significant challenges with work-life balance. Participant D cited life

experiences such as being a “mom of two” and pursuing social work as a “second career” while

managing situations such as a “family emergency.” In a similar way, the challenges of transition

into the MSW program and handling expectations felt to Participant B like “jumping into a great

big pool with both feet.”

The second subtheme is isolation in online learning. Remote education presented unique

barriers to connection. Participant A reflected on being fully remote, noting the experience of

“talking to the whole room” in the classroom setup and how this can make it difficult to form

personal connections. Participant B mentioned the “learning curve” that comes with university

systems and technology after being away from school for “about a decade.”

A final subtheme is low margin of error. For these students, the stakes of the MSW

journey can become high. Participant C weighed the investment required to commit to the

academic program and asked, “Is it worth it?” Another example is a lack of capacity to use

support systems, even when they are needed and known about. Participant D discussed being

their own “blocker,” noting that balancing their “full life” makes it difficult to access university

resources, including those that could support academic persistence.

Peer Support versus Formal Mentorship

The third and final theme explores how students fill in the gaps left by formal institutions

and systems through informal networks. This pattern shows how multiple layers of support can

work together to address gaps. In response to the limitations of formal support, students often

turn to other personal connections for support.

21

A first subtheme is peer connection. In the absence of a formal match, students turned to

one another. Participant D mentioned “texting classmates” to track responsibilities and cope with

stressors through “solidarity.” Similarly, Participant E emphasized the value of “large group

projects” in establishing “trust” and “empathy” among peers.

The second subtheme is near-peer mentoring. There is some desire for guidance from

those just a few steps ahead in the MSW journey. Participant B suggested exploring a “peer

mentorship program” similar to what they have observed in the corporate setting, while also

expressing value in learning from students who are earlier in the program, who have a “refreshed

mind” and greater comfort with technology.

A final subtheme is self-reliance and autonomy. Many students, such as Participant E,

have drawn from prior experiences in which they “powered through” challenges. Participant D

often felt on their own with professional decisions, such as ensuring “safety” and managing

“complex behaviors” in the field. The resulting emotional pressure from this situation and similar

ones is evident in various descriptions, such as when Participant A “freaked out for a couple of

hours” during a stressful moment, and being left to “figure out on my own.”

Discussion and Implications

The data and its analysis lead to an explanation of what they mean for MSW students and

the field of social work as a whole. A connection can be made between the lived experiences of

the research participants, critical pedagogy, and the existing literature. This results in practical

ideas for developing more equitable and accessible pathways to mentorship in social work

education and training.

Interpretation of Findings

22

The findings from this study suggest that for first-year MSW students at a small private

university, the status quo for mentorship is an informal, faculty-dependent, peer-driven

experience rather than a structured program. While the faculty can be very accessible in this type

of setting, a notable gap in mentorship exists, especially for non-traditional students and remote

students. The results can be further interpreted in relation to the research question, themes,

subthemes, and unique responses.

First, in the absence of a formal mentorship program, students tend to view professors as

the primary influence on their professional identity. The role of faculty can be seen as being a

first responder in addition to being an instructor. As described by Participant C, a professor can

be seen as a student’s “number one person” for navigating and transitioning into the profession.

The power of responsiveness is evident in how Participant A labeled the “spot on” culture within

the university, showing how quick communication with faculty can boost the self-assurance of

students and reduce anxiety. Observing this theme and its subthemes demonstrates what students

value most in their connection with professors. Although support with administrative tasks is

needed and appreciated, these relationships shine the most when students can seek a professor as

a role model whose career path they can look up to. This suggests that mentorship is most

effective when it goes beyond daily logistics and offers guidance on a student’s career vision.

Second, students face a tight window of expectations as they balance academics with

additional responsibilities, such as attending remotely, working, parenting, or pivoting careers.

Remote students, such as Participant A, face the intimidating scenario of “talking to the whole

room” through a screen in class, which creates a barrier to the organic connections that would

otherwise be made face-to-face. Reflecting on feedback from Participant D, students often act as

their own “blockers,” not due to lack of interest or effort, but because their “full life” leaves little

23

to no room for seeking out help. The existential question posed by Participant C, “Is it worth it?”

speaks to how the heavy investment of an MSW program can lead to burnout or withdrawal

without psychosocial support.

Lastly, students confront gaps in knowledge and experience through informal

connections and peer networks. Participant D’s circumstances illustrate how students turn to

their peers for “solidarity,” which serves as a protective factor against stress. There is also an

untapped demand for near-peer mentorship, as stated by Participant B, making the most of

“varying levels of experience” both ways and being encouraged by a “refreshed mind” to

navigate hurdles. Participant E demonstrated the burden of students having to rely on themselves

at times, facing intense pressure as they simply “powered through” challenges without guidance.

This highlights a clear need to shift the burden of support from the individual student to a more

formal system.

Connection to Theory and Literature

The findings of this study relate to the theory of critical pedagogy and generally align

with and expand the existing literature on MSW mentorship. The student experience can be

better understood through the lens of critical pedagogy, which enhances the results. After

studying students' academic challenges, the evidence points to the larger issues of self-

determination and professional empowerment.

Critical pedagogy helps frame the student’s reliance on faculty and peers as collaborative

learning rather than the traditional “banking” style of education. One application of this

framework is to connect the concept of praxis with professional identity. The findings show that

students seek mentorship that promotes a vision for their careers rather than just administrative

support. This matches critical pedagogy’s integration of reflection and action, and it’s a path for

24

students to translate theory into practice through role models. Critical pedagogy also plays a role

by addressing power imbalances and inequality. Through this lens, the additional barriers faced

by remote and non-traditional students are identified as systemic issues rather than individual

failures. In agreement with the literature, the current study reveals how the status quo and a lack

of mentorship disproportionately affect the students who already face an uphill climb in social

work education and training.

The results of this study are largely consistent with existing research, but with a unique

focus on the small private university setting. Similar to prior studies, the mentorship gap

emerged as an overlooked aspect of MSW training, with many students lacking a formal match

(Pomeroy & Steiker, 2011; Skrzypek et al., 2019). The findings also reinforce that non-

traditional students especially benefit from the psychosocial support of mentorship, rather than

just academic tutoring (Jensen, 2017; Schuman et al., 2021). As in other settings, the protective

value of peer support was evident as well (Mitschke et al., 2021).

In contrast, the results of this study differ with others in a couple of ways. The personal

nature of faculty connections was emphasized in the small-university setting, as opposed to the

administrative focus in other research. There was clear value in effective, efficient

communication to promote students' self-efficacy. This research also placed a greater emphasis

on barriers at the individual level than at the organizational level. Existing literature often cites

the challenges associated with a lack of resources within the organization, while these

participants noted their own circumstances as barriers. This suggests that sources of mentorship

can remain inaccessible when detached from the student’s academic experience.

By applying critical pedagogy, this study presents mentorship as a tool for equity.

Although the value of mentorship is universal, access to it is not universal among all students.

25

This research addresses that gap in the small-university context by examining how students

navigate these systemic barriers, rely on their own efforts to persist academically, and seek out a

sense of mentorship even when it is not formally provided.

Implications

The findings of this study call for action within MSW academic programs and beyond.

Such institutions should consider the heavy reliance emerging social work practitioners have on

informal support networks and intervene accordingly. There is a clear opportunity to transform

support models from reactive to proactive. Pursuing structured, intentional mentorship

approaches offers practical advantages over the status quo.

Practice implications prompt social work practitioners, including university faculty, field

supervisors, and other experienced professionals, to focus on education and training that are both

developmental and administrative for supervisees. One suggestion is to establish intentional

mentorship check-ins. Instead of waiting for social work trainees to reach out, practitioners

should initiate dialogue on career vision that links short-term strategy to long-term goals. A

responsive approach to communication also maintains prompt, empathetic relationships with

early social workers, who can draw on role models for self-efficacy. Practitioners should also

acknowledge, validate, and normalize the challenges of navigating professional development

while balancing life circumstances.

Policy implications call for structural changes at all three levels of social work practice.

At the micro level, policies should be established to embed mentorship into the first-year MSW

student experience. Considering how students face barriers to accessing resources, support

through a credited or required program feature would ensure inclusion across all student

backgrounds. At the mezzo level, academic social work departments should implement formal

26

near-peer mentoring programs. This shifts the burden away from faculty and creates a nested

support system where students at different phases of the MSW curriculum can share knowledge

and solidarity. At the macro level, accreditation standards, such as those of the CSWE, should

adopt metrics for mentorship accessibility to ensure equitable support across all MSW programs.

Research implications include gaps that require further attention. Future research should

track individuals' long-term persistence beyond first-year education and graduation, measuring

the correlation between early interventions and long-term career retention. As the existing

literature describes, this study further underscores the need to examine mentors' experiences.

Faculty who currently provide guidance and peer mentors who will provide guidance in future

programs that are designed and implemented should be consulted to better understand their

perspectives on this mutual learning process. Additionally, comparative studies should be

conducted to evaluate the best methods for service delivery. Sources of mentorship built into the

MSW curriculum can be compared with sources that students opt into to measure the

effectiveness of each.

Limitations

An interpretation of the findings must also consider the several limitations of this study,

which provide context for this research and set the stage for future work. The main limitation is

the small sample size, since having only five first-year MSW students participate narrows the

range of lived experiences. The results also have limited generalizability, as they are centered on

a single small private university in the Midwestern United States and may not apply to larger

public institutions or to academic programs in other regions. A lack of participant diversity also

limits the findings. The purposive sample did not reflect a wide range of demographics and life

circumstances, and students from marginalized backgrounds may be underrepresented for

27

reasons similar to what was described in the discussion of the non-traditional student experience.

The use of semi-structured interviews introduced additional limitations, such as question design

that can limit the range of responses, researcher bias in data interpretation, and shortcomings of

virtual data collection via video conferencing. A cross-sectional study was the final limitation, as

it provided only a snapshot of student perceptions and experiences.

Conclusion

This study explored the inner workings of mentorship within an MSW academic

program, including the gap between its recognized value and the lack of a formal system to

provide it. The discussion is shaped by the complexity of professional identity and academic

persistence. With these factors in mind, the study’s core findings can contribute to social work

education and guide future inquiry.

Summary of Key Points

The insights gained from this study involve the status quo and a future vision. Mentorship

currently functions as an informal, faculty-dependent, peer-driven experience rather than as a

structured or required program. Students view professors as their most critical connection to

professional identity, valuing responsive communication and career role modeling over

administrative assistance alone. Non-traditional students, who often balance multiple roles, face

significant barriers, including limited access to resources and high stakes in their education and

training. Looking ahead, psychosocial support and solidarity can be drawn from near-peer

relationships and informal peer connections that students already explore and establish in the

absence of formal mentor matches. This study highlights a clear demand for shifting the burden

of mentorship from the individual student to the institution through formal, proactive support

models.

28

Contribution to the Field

This study contributes to the field of social work education by shifting the focus from

organizational resource constraints to the lived, subjective experiences of MSW students in a

small private university setting. While previous research has outlined the value of mentorship in

great detail, this study offers several unique perspectives. Unlike the existing literature’s focus

on institutional failures, this research extends the discussion to the complex lives of non-

traditional students and the resulting barriers to seeking support. Resources can remain

inaccessible unless there is a proactive effort to integrate them into the student’s academic

experience. This study also reveals new insight into the high value students place on responsive

communication with professors who serve as more than just instructors. The findings discover an

unmet demand among students for near-peer mentorship with the potential for more sustainable,

comprehensive support. Applying critical pedagogy in this study also further advances the role of

mentorship in social justice. Through this lens, the inequity in the current mentorship system can

be addressed through praxis to connect classroom theory with students' future professional

identities.

Suggestions for Future Research

The findings and limitations of this study lead to several suggestions for future

exploration to further support mentorship in social work education. Longitudinal studies on

career retention would examine how efforts in the academic setting correlate with outcomes in

the social work field, such as preventing burnout. Comparative studies of different types of

institutions, such as small private universities versus large public ones, would evaluate the role of

faculty across settings. Future research should explore feedback from mentors, such as faculty,

alumni, and near-peers, to understand the barriers posed by resource and time constraints in

29

developing sustainable mentorship programs. Future studies would benefit from measuring the

efficacy of integrated mentorship models versus optional models, particularly for non-traditional

students, who may encounter significant hurdles. Subsequent research should recruit a more

diverse sample that includes students from underrepresented backgrounds, aligning with the

principles of critical pedagogy and intersectionality. Considering the isolation reported by remote

students in this study, a closer look at e-mentoring strategies could help supplement the existing

literature by bridging the gap between virtual learning and relationship-building.
